<TITLE> UZBEKISTAN SIGNS NATO PARTNERSHIP DEAL</TITLE>
<HEADLINE> UZBEKISTAN SIGNS NATO PARTNERSHIP DEAL</HEADLINE>
<SUBJECT> BC-NATO-UZBEKISTAN </SUBJECT>
<AUTHOR></AUTHOR>
<TEXT>
<NOTES></NOTES>
<DATELINE>BRUSSELS (Reuter) </DATELINE>
<P>  The central Asian state of Uzbekistan became the 22nd country to sign NATO's partnership program Wednesday. </P>
<P> Uzbek Foreign Minister Saidmukhtar Saidkasimov told reporters after signing the Partnership for Peace (PFP) framework document he hoped it would encourage a climate of peace in his area. </P>
<P> ``We hope this will strengthen peace and stability both in Central Asia and in Europe,'' he said. </P>
<P> NATO Deputy Secretary-General Sergio Balanzino said dialogue between the 16-nation alliance and Central Asian states was an important element for fostering security and stability in the Eurasian area. </P>
<P> ``Building a stable security environment in this region is a common task which we can fulfil only by joining our efforts,'' Balanzino said. </P>
<P> A NATO delegation is expected to visit Uzbekistan July 21 to hold concrete discussions on a future relationship between the alliance and the Central Asian state. </P>
<P> Balanzino said the Uzbek government had shown particular interest in gaining NATO's help with training officers. </P>
<P> Under PFP, partners can also consult with NATO if they perceive a direct threat to their territorial integrity, political independence or security. </P>
