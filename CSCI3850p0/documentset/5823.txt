<TITLE> Juppe, speaking for 21 states, urges indefinite NPT</TITLE>
<HEADLINE> Juppe, speaking for 21 states, urges indefinite NPT</HEADLINE>
<SUBJECT> BC-UN-NUCLEAR-JUPPE </SUBJECT>
<AUTHOR>     By Anthony Goodman </AUTHOR>
<TEXT>
<NOTES></NOTES>
<DATELINE>UNITED NATIONS (Reuter) </DATELINE>
<P>  French Foreign Minister Alain Juppe, speaking on behalf of a bloc of 21 countries, called Tuesday for the indefinite extension of the 1970 Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT), designed to prevent the spread of nuclear weapons. </P>
<P> A simple majority of the 178 signatories of the treaty -- 90 votes -- is needed for indefinite, unconditional renewal. </P>
<P> Juppe, speaking for the 15-nation European Union and six East and Central European states associated with it, represented nearly a quarter of the required total vote. </P>
<P> ``It is in the interest of the international community to counter the proliferation of nuclear weapons. It is in its interest to promote access to the peaceful uses of nuclear energy. It is in its interest that the process of disarmament be continued. These are the three essential aspects of the NPT,'' he told a conference on renewing the 25-year-old accord. </P>
<P> A number of Third World countries feel the five acknowledged nuclear powers -- the United States, Russia, Britain, China and France -- have not done enough to pursue nuclear disarmament, as is also required under the NPT. They want it renewed for a fixed period or periods. </P>
<P> To be convincing, and in order not to weaken the treaty, supporters of indefinite extension hope to muster more than a bare majority when a decision is taken toward the end of the month-long conference. </P>
<P> Referring to the nuclear weapon states, Juppe said; ``We solemnly reaffirm our commitment, in accordance with Article VI (of the NPT), to continue negotiations in good faith on effective measures pertaining to nuclear disarmament which remains our ultimate goal.'' </P>
<P> While the NPT was enjoying increasing success, ``new regional ambitions are surfacing for which the acquisition of weapons of mass destruction could constitute particularly dangerous means of domination,'' he warned. </P>
<P> ``We have to do our utmost to reduce the risks of uncertainty and instability, to increase confidence among states without which there is no security, and to strengthen the international legal status of the NPT by making it permanent ... </P>
